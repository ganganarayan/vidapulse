How do agencies justify video retainers?

Agencies justify video retainers by connecting the work to the client's revenue rather than to activity. Each improvement to a video lifts one of two things you can measure: the share of viewers who reach the offer, or the share who convert once they do. Both feed directly into the client's sales, so a documented before-and-after on retention, reach-to-offer, and conversions becomes the case for the retainer. Tie the work to revenue, not activity

Retainers come under pressure when the client cannot see what they are paying for. A report full of edits made and hours spent describes activity; it does not explain whether the video is selling better. The way to defend a retainer is to reframe every deliverable in terms the client already cares about — viewers reaching the offer, and viewers converting.

Those two measures sit directly upstream of revenue. More viewers reaching the offer means more people hear the ask; more of them converting means more sales from the same traffic. When you can point to a lift in either, you are no longer justifying the retainer with effort — you are justifying it with the result the client hired you for. That shift is the whole argument.

Show the before-and-after that proves the lift

The strongest justification is a comparison the client can verify. Capture the video's retention curve, reach-to-offer, and conversions before a change, make a focused edit, then capture them again on fresh traffic. The difference is the evidence the retainer produced something.

  • Retention — a flatter drop and longer average watch time after the edit.
  • Reach-to-offer — a larger share of viewers now reaching the ask.
  • Conversions — with conversion and CTA tracking (Pro), more of those viewers clicking through.

Place the before and after side by side, annotate the section you changed, and the client sees cause and effect in one view. This is far more persuasive than a dashboard of ad metrics that never explains the why, and it is exactly the kind of evidence that makes a renewal conversation easy.

Translate the lift into client revenue terms

A percentage lift lands harder when you connect it to money. Walk the client through the chain from the measured improvement to their own numbers, using their actual traffic and offer rather than invented figures.

Hypothetical illustration, not real data: suppose a client sends the same volume of traffic to a VSL each month, and your edit lifts the share reaching the offer from a quarter to a third. With everything else held constant, a meaningfully larger group now hears the ask each month, and at the client's own conversion rate and price that turns into additional sales the client can calculate themselves. The exact numbers are theirs to fill in — your job is to show the lift and the chain it feeds.

Keep the money in the client's hands: you supply the measured change and the logic, and the client applies their own price and conversion rate. That keeps the case honest and makes the revenue impact feel like the client's own conclusion rather than your sales pitch.

Make the evidence routine

A retainer is justified continuously, not once. Build the before-and-after into a regular cadence so each cycle of work produces fresh proof. Run the find-fix-remeasure loop, export or screenshot the curves and reach-to-offer figures, and put them in the monthly review next to the change you made.

Use UTM and source attribution to show the lift holds across the traffic the client is paying for, so the result is not an artefact of one source. Over several months the accumulated before-and-afters become a track record — the clearest possible answer to "is this retainer worth it?"
